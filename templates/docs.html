Getting started

  • If you haven't already, create an account and find a team (of up to four people)! Sign up in the top right corner of the screen.
  • Go to your Profile, which can be found after logging in by clicking on your name in the top right in the dropdown.
  • There you should find your developer key (which you should NOT share with anyone), and your current matchmaking rating. You will need your dev key for testing your AI on our server.
  • If anything doesn't make sense, please email Darwin Ding (dingd@rpi.edu) with any questions you may have!

Premise

In some dystopian, robotic future, you're just a colony trying to survive. Conveniently placed across three horizontally aligned lanes is another colony, which sucks and must be destroyed. This 1600 x 600 pixel grid is only large enough for one of you, after all.

The game this time around is a 1v1 tower defense that takes place over the course of five minutes. Each player begins with a core, which are the main objectives to take out in this game. Each player additionally has a smaller objective tower in each lane. After five minutes, if both main cores are still standing, a tie-breaker process occurs to determine a winner.

You can check out the code on github.

The game progresses in real-time at a rate of 30 FPS, where players can input up to a single action per frame. These actions can either consist of buying a tower in one of the plots that exist along each of the lanes, or buying a troop to progress down any of the lanes. Note that you don't actually directly control the actions of any particular unit after its been created, they all have set AIs. You'll find that just managing where/when to buy them will still be quite a handful!

You buy troops/towers using bits, the local currency. You get these bits every second depending on what your income is (which can be increased/decreased throughout the course of the game). You have a base income of 500 bits/sec when the game starts.

Troops 101

Troops are the main game objects you will need to worry about, as they'll comprise the majority of your attack and defense. Here you'll get an introduction to all of the troops you'll have to play with.

Troops have each of the following characteristics that you'll need to worry about:

  • X, Y: Pretty self explanatory. Troops have an X, Y position that varies from 0-1599 for X and 0-599 for Y. Player 1 (blue, left player)'s units spawn at x = 0, and player 2 (red, right player)'s units spawn at x = 1599.
  • HP/MaxHP: Pretty self explanatory. Troops keep track of their maximum HP as well as their current running HP. If it goes to 0 or below that troop is considered dead and will be cleaned up at the end of the frame.
  • Enum: Each troop and tower has an enum value that tells the game what type of unit it is.
  • Damage: Most troops are single-target shooters (as opposed to AOE), and damage is how much damage this troop will do in a single step.
  • Cost: You must have at least X bits and Y income to purchase this unit on a given turn. The vast majority of troops simply cost a bit value.
  • Speed: This troop acts every (30/speed) frames. Higher speed means it moves more frequently.
  • Stride: When this troop chooses to move, it will move (stride) pixels in the direction of its player. If you are the blue player, the troop will move to the right. If red, left.
  • Additionally, some troops have special characteristics that separate them from the crowd. Make sure to read all of the troops below!

After you decide you want to buy a specific troop, and you know you have the bits and income to support the purchase, you'll have to decide on which lane you want to place it in. Finally, once you submit the buy action, you're good to go! The troop has its own AI that it will follows, and that will be described below.

Nut

Nuts are standard foot-soldiers with below average HP, average stride/reach and high speed/damage. This unit is great for high DPS (damage per second) but needs to be supplemented with meatier troops. The Nut is the cheapest unit in the game, and the most cost efficient.

Nuts move super simply -- each step they try to find a target in range to shoot, and move otherwise. If they have a valid target that is neither dead nor out of range, they will shoot it. They will always prioritize the closest tower or troop it sees first.

Towers 101

Your colony can't just thrive on troops, however! You'll be placing towers in the plots alongside each lane to supplement your army.

Towers typically cost income as well as a raw bit value. Note that the income you spend on towers is returned to you when the tower dies (the bits are not, though).

Towers have each of the following characteristics that you'll need to worry about:

  • X, Y: Pretty self explanatory. Towers also have X, Y just like troops, although they are stationary and can only be spawned in very specific plots. There is a side-lane of plots 50px above and 50px below each troop lane. Plots are 100 pixels wide and go from 300 pixels to 1300 pixels in each side-lane. (As a result, there's 11 plots per side-lane, two side-lanes per lane, six total side-lanes and 66 total plots).
  • HP/MaxHP: Pretty self explanatory. Towers also keep track of their maximum HP as well as their current running HP. If it goes to 0 or below that tower is considered dead and will be cleaned up at the end of the frame.
  • Enum: Each troop and tower has an enum value that tells the game what type of unit it is.
  • Damage: If a tower deals damage (and there are many that do and do not), this is how much damage this troop will do in a single step.
  • Cost: You must have at least X bits and Y income to purchase this unit on a given turn. Towers pretty much all require an upfront bit cost and also an income cost.
  • Speed: This tower acts every (30/speed) frames. Higher speed means it moves more frequently.

Just like with troops, you submit a buy action for a tower in a specific plot. Note that you can only build plots within your territory, which will be explained later. After the tower is created, it'll run on its own AI, so you don't have to worry about coding one for it!

Territory

Your towers can only be constructed within your territory, which shifts as the game goes on.

You have three territory ranges, one for each lane. These default to half of the lane for each player. Note that there are 11 plots in each lane, but by default no one can build in the middle plot (effectively there can only be 10 towers out at any point in time).

Your territory in a lane increases by 200 pixels when you kill your opponent's lane objective, and decreases when yours is destroyed. If both objective towers are gone, both players will again share half the lane. Note that if your lane objective is destroyed and you still have a tower standing in territory you will be losing, those towers will be instantly destroyed.

You don't have to calculate your plots, because we actually pass you the plots you can build in. This is explained better in the Sample Output, but this information is contained in the "towers" field in the player objects.

Fog of War

Fog of war is a mechanic where you can't always see the entirety of the battlefield. This adds a little layer of complexity where you'll have to fight to figure out what your opponent is doing.

To prevent games from being inevitable stalemates if a player chooses to simply mimic their opponent's moves, we've generated a fog of war that masks the areas of the battlegrounds you can't see. By default, you can always see the extent of your territory (which by default is your half of the field). You can also see as far as your furthest unit's range. It should be noted that if your furthest unit in the Top lane can see up to x = 1400 (assume that you are the left player), then you can see up to x = 1400 in all three lanes, even if your Top lane furthest unit would not normally be able to fire into the Bottom lane.

The extent of your vision range is called your horizon bounds. The horizonMin and horizonMax for both players are given in the output from the server.

When playing the game manually on our website, you will be able to see the fog of war visually with the gray section that shifts as your vision shifts.

Troops that are outside your horizon bounds simply do not show up in the output, while towers outside your horizon bounds will come up as JSON objects with enum -3 and owner equal to your opponent's player value (see sample output for details)

Game Constants

Game Orientation (0, 0) is the bottom left corner and (1600, 600) is the top right corner of the screen.
Game Dimensions 1600px (width) by 600px (height)
Plot Dimensions Every 100 pixels from x = 300px to x = 1300px.
As a result, there are 11 plots per side-lane, two side-lanes per lane and overall 66 plots (from 00 to 65).
Side-lanes There is a side-lane 50px above and below each lane.
Lane Constants Top-lane is at y = 475, mid-lane is at y = 280, and bot-lane is at y = 100.
Objective Constants Player 1's objectives spawn at x = 200, and player 2's objectives spawn at x = 1400.
Territory Your territory defaults to exactly halfway between the main cores.

Engine details

Each frame, the engine goes through a very predescribed set of actions in order to resolve game states as fairly as possible.

The steps are as follows:

  • Player inputs are read and resolved. If towers/troops are bought (and they can be afforded), they are spawned.
  • Each tower and troop decides what they are going to do, or if they can even do anything (this depends on their speed). (This is a preparation stage, and no tower/troop actually acts here.)
  • All towers and troops iterate.
  • Towers and troops are cleaned up if they have sub-zero HP.
  • If a player's main core is seen to be dead, the game terminates.

    Developing your AI

    You're essentially building a websocket client, which pretty much all languages are capable of. If you're having trouble getting started, please feel free to expand off of the Sample AIs we've provided (below). We're going to keep expanding the list of sample AIs to try to cover the languages we think people will be developing in.

    The workflow is as follows:

    • Your client will create a connection to either ws://npcompete.io/wsplay (for matchmaking, which will match you against the next opponent that does this), or to ws://npcompete.io/wsjoin?game=GAMENAME (for joining a specific lobby, which will match you against the next player who joins that lobby as well).
    • Once the connection is established, you need to send a message which is simply your devkey! Your devkey will be used to identify you for the competition, so make sure to know yours and not share it!
    • While the websocket is open, feel free to send moves at your own volition! The game will be sending output at a rate of 30 FPS. This output will contain the game board state that you can see.

    Player objects are pretty straightforward as well, they contain an "owner" property that tells you whether they are player 1 (coming from left, blue team) or player 2 (coming from right, red team). Players have income, as mentioned before, as well as bits. They also have "towers", which is an array of tower unit objects, and "troops", which is an array of troop unit objects. Players finally have a "mainCore" JSON object that represents the main core. The "name" field is the player's username.

    Although calculatable by the players, we've provided the horizon and territory bounds in the form of territoryMin/Max and horizonMin/Max. These are ints that show the bounds of your territory and horizon respectively. Your territory dictates which plots you can physically build towers in, and defaults to your half of the field. Your horizon is the range at which you can see. Everything else is blanketed by the fog of war (see the Fog of War section above).

    Troops is also an array of JSON troop unit objects. These are similar in structure to tower unit objects -- they have x/y/owner/maxhp/hp and enum values. This array, however, is of variable length but it is guaranteed to be sorted first by y value (descending value, essentially what lane the unit is in), and then by x value (ascending order). Remember that x=0, y=0 is the bottom left corner and x=1599,y=599 is the top right corner.

    Troops outside of your horizon bounds (inside the fog of war) will also not show up.
